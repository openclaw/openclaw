# **Configuration Card 51**

## **Culture Shift Signals Loop (Role-Segmented, Non-Evaluative)**

---

### **1\. Configuration Name**

**Culture Shift Signals Loop (Role-Segmented, Non-Evaluative)**

---

### **2\. Context Snapshot**

This configuration is used when an organization wants to track culture shifts without reducing people to engagement scores. It supports safe-to-speak pattern monitoring across role levels while avoiding public ranking or forced agreement.

---

### **3\. Primary Intent**

**Learning & improvement**

---

### **4\. Secondary Intent (Optional)**

Ethics, risk & accountability

---

### **5\. Population of Interest**

- Multiple groups with different relationships to power (frontline, managers, leaders, support functions)

---

### **6\. Recruitment & Participation Mode**

- Recruitment: segmented invitation by cohort and role

- Participation: **mixed**
  - Asynchronous reflection pulses (weekly or biweekly)

  - Optional facilitated Rooms for pattern reflection

---

### **7\. Sensemaking Interaction Pattern**

- Individual self-interpretation first

- Pattern reflection within and across cohorts

- Facilitation used to hold tension, not force consensus

---

### **8\. Spryng Containers Used**

- **Groups** (role-based cohorts)

- Optional **Insiders** (longitudinal cohort for trend continuity)

- Optional **Rooms** (cross-role interpretation sessions)

---

### **9\. Roles Present**

- Participants (across levels)

- Facilitator(s)

- Account Admin

- Decision-makers as listeners, not judges

---

### **10\. Psychological Safety Posture**

- Safety often **uneven** across role levels

- Anonymous or pseudonymous defaults recommended for lower-power cohorts

- No attribution of statements to individuals in shared outputs

---

### **11\. Agreement & Governance Posture**

- Spryng Terms of Service

- Program participation consent

- Explicit non-evaluative clause (not for performance review)

- Time-bound governance window with retention and deletion rules

---

### **12\. What Emerged**

- Shifts in trust and psychological safety by cohort

- Recurring friction patterns and contradiction clusters

- Early warning signals before visible culture failure

---

### **13\. Decisions Supported (If Any)**

- Sequencing of leadership interventions

- Changes in rituals, communication norms, or manager support

- Adjustment of governance boundaries for sensitive dialogue

---

### **14\. What Remained Unresolved**

- Persistent contradictions across levels

- Tension between speed of change and safety

- Aporia remained valid in high-uncertainty zones

---

### **15\. Reflections & Cautions**

- Do not publish comparative league tables by team

- Do not convert pattern outputs into HR scoring

- Treat disagreement as a signal, not a defect

---

# **Configuration Card 52**

## **AI Adoption Readiness (Human Work, Risk, and Accountability)**

---

### **1\. Configuration Name**

**AI Adoption Readiness (Human Work, Risk, and Accountability)**

---

### **2\. Context Snapshot**

This configuration is used when organizations are introducing AI tools and need to understand readiness, concern patterns, and governance gaps without using a compliance-only survey posture.

---

### **3\. Primary Intent**

**Ethics, risk & accountability**

---

### **4\. Secondary Intent (Optional)**

Learning & improvement

---

### **5\. Population of Interest**

- Multiple stakeholder groups affected by AI use (operators, managers, compliance, product, legal, customers or community reps)

---

### **6\. Recruitment & Participation Mode**

- Recruitment: role-segmented invitation plus targeted opt-in for impacted groups

- Participation: **mixed**
  - Asynchronous story capture and self-interpretation

  - Optional facilitated Rooms for governance interpretation

---

### **7\. Sensemaking Interaction Pattern**

- Participants interpret their own experience of AI-assisted work

- Cross-role pattern interpretation highlights risk asymmetries

- No requirement to reach one shared narrative

---

### **8\. Spryng Containers Used**

- **Groups** (by role and business function)

- **Insiders** (governance subgroup over time)

- Optional **Rooms** (risk and ethics interpretation sessions)

---

### **9\. Roles Present**

- Participants

- Facilitator(s)

- AI governance lead

- DPO or legal/compliance representative

- Decision-makers

---

### **10\. Psychological Safety Posture**

- Safety may be **fragile** where job security concerns are active

- Uneven voice confidence across role levels

- Protective anonymity or pseudonymity often needed for truthful reporting

---

### **11\. Agreement & Governance Posture**

- Spryng Terms of Service

- Program participation consent

- Clear scope statement: not for individual surveillance or performance judgment

- Data minimization and role-limited access rules

---

### **12\. What Emerged**

- Readiness variation by function and role

- Risk hotspots in workflow handoffs

- Ethical tension patterns (accuracy, transparency, bias, accountability)

---

### **13\. Decisions Supported (If Any)**

- Pilot scope and rollout pacing

- Human oversight and escalation protocol design

- Training priorities and governance checkpoints

---

### **14\. What Remained Unresolved**

- Uncertainty around acceptable automation boundaries

- Ongoing disagreement on trade-offs between speed and assurance

- Deferred decisions on high-risk use cases

---

### **15\. Reflections & Cautions**

- Avoid labeling teams as "AI ready" or "not ready" using simplistic scores

- Keep outputs at pattern level, not person-level judgment

- Re-run in cycles as adoption conditions change

---

Crisis, repair & healing

---

---

### **6\. Recruitment & Participation Mode**

- Participation: **mixed**
  - Asynchronous participation first for broad signal capture

  - Optional facilitated Rooms by integration theme

- Cross-cohort interpretation of overlap and divergence

- Facilitation focused on safe comparison without blame

---

### **8\. Spryng Containers Used**

- Optional **Insiders** (integration watch group over time)

- Optional **Rooms** (targeted trust-repair and coordination dialogues)

---

### **9\. Roles Present**

- Participants across both legacy organizations

- Facilitator(s)

- Integration program lead

- Account Admin

- Decision-makers for policy/process integration

---

### **10\. Psychological Safety Posture**

- Safety often **fragile** during identity and role uncertainty

- Uneven confidence to speak across legacy affiliation

- Anonymous or pseudonymous options improve candor early on

---

### **11\. Agreement & Governance Posture**

- Spryng Terms of Service

- Integration program consent

- Confidentiality and non-retaliation commitments

- Explicit non-evaluative use statement

---

### **12\. What Emerged**

- Friction hotspots at workflow and cultural boundaries

- Trust asymmetries between cohorts

- Reintegration opportunities with low-regret interventions

---

### **13\. Decisions Supported (If Any)**

- Integration sequencing and support allocation

- Changes to handoffs, role clarity, and communication protocols

- Timing for leadership interventions and follow-up listening cycles

---

### **14\. What Remained Unresolved**

- Legacy identity tensions not yet reconciled

- Conflicting narratives of fairness and loss

- Deferred policy decisions requiring more evidence

---

### **15\. Reflections & Cautions**

- Do not force symbolic closure before operational trust is rebuilt

- Avoid public comparisons that increase defensiveness

- Repeat in cycles; one-time capture is not enough in reintegration contexts

- Partner participation agreement

- Cross-partner friction patterns in handoffs and expectations

- Recurring channel health constraints and trust bottlenecks

- Improvement opportunities requiring shared governance, not unilateral fixes

---

- Partner enablement priorities and support model changes

- Governance adjustments for escalation and accountability

- Tension between partner autonomy and standardization

- Commercially sensitive disagreements that require phased handling

- Some policy decisions deferred to later governance cycles

- Keep improvement and accountability linked but distinct

- Revisit quarterly to maintain federated trust
