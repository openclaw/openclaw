# Visual directions

Choose one direction based on product purpose, audience, content density, and brand character. Adapt it rather than copying every trait.

## 1. Operations console

Best for infrastructure, security, monitoring, support, and internal admin systems.

- Dense but calm information architecture.
- Neutral surfaces with restrained semantic colors.
- Compact controls, strong alignment, tabular numerals, visible timestamps and units.
- Persistent filters, saved views, bulk actions, status history, and clear ownership.
- Motion is minimal and functional.
- Avoid decorative dashboards that hide real data behind oversized cards.

## 2. Editorial precision

Best for reports, knowledge products, research, professional services, and content-heavy websites.

- Strong type hierarchy, generous reading width, deliberate whitespace.
- A serif or expressive display face can pair with a highly readable text face.
- Rules, captions, pull quotes, and asymmetrical grids create structure.
- Color is used sparingly for emphasis.
- Avoid excessive cards; let content sections form the architecture.

## 3. Warm productivity

Best for collaboration tools, workflow products, education, and approachable SaaS.

- Friendly but disciplined typography.
- Soft neutrals, one confident accent, modest radii, limited elevation.
- Helpful empty states and clear onboarding.
- Microcopy should reduce uncertainty and explain consequences.
- Avoid childish illustration or excessive softness in high-stakes workflows.

## 4. Premium dark

Best for media, creative tools, AI workspaces, developer products, and presentation-led experiences.

- Deep neutral background with carefully controlled luminance steps.
- One or two high-chroma accents; avoid rainbow status palettes.
- Crisp type, subtle borders, and strong focus treatment.
- Use glow only to signal active state or depth.
- Validate contrast carefully; dark gray on black is often unreadable.

## 5. Modern institutional

Best for government, finance, healthcare administration, enterprise portals, and public services.

- Trustworthy typography, clear navigation, predictable controls, strong accessibility.
- White or light neutral foundation with a stable primary color.
- Explicit labels, status wording, timestamps, audit details, and policy context.
- Responsive behavior must support older devices and text enlargement.
- Avoid trendy visual effects that reduce clarity or perceived reliability.

## 6. Neo-brutalist utility

Best for bold campaigns, portfolios, event pages, experimental products, and youth-oriented brands.

- High contrast, visible structure, strong borders, oversized type, deliberate rawness.
- Limited palette and clear geometric rhythm.
- Interactions can feel tactile but must remain accessible.
- Use only when the brand can support confrontation and visual tension.
- Avoid applying this to dense enterprise workflows without strong reason.

## 7. Quiet luxury

Best for hospitality, architecture, high-end retail, premium services, and personal brands.

- Restrained palette, excellent imagery, low visual noise, precise spacing.
- Typography and composition carry the brand more than effects.
- Use subtle transitions and high-quality crop behavior.
- Calls to action remain clear even when visually understated.
- Avoid generic beige minimalism with weak hierarchy.

## 8. Data narrative

Best for analytics, annual reports, public dashboards, market intelligence, and explainers.

- The story controls chart order and annotation.
- Use a consistent scale, legend, unit, and comparison baseline.
- Pair summaries with drill-down details.
- Color encodes meaning, not decoration.
- Avoid unlabeled charts, truncated axes, and dashboards that require users to infer the conclusion.

## Direction selection questions

Before choosing, answer:

1. Is this primarily for frequent work, occasional decisions, reading, or persuasion?
2. How much information must be visible at once?
3. What should the user feel: trust, speed, confidence, curiosity, warmth, or exclusivity?
4. What visual constraints already exist in the brand or product?
5. Which devices and environments matter most?
6. What would make the design inappropriate for the audience?

## Typography guidance

- Use one family when it provides enough hierarchy; use two only with a clear role split.
- Prefer variable fonts when already available, but do not add large font payloads casually.
- Set readable line length and line height before styling secondary details.
- Use tabular numerals for metrics, money, durations, timestamps, and aligned tables.
- Avoid using weight alone to distinguish every level; combine size, spacing, and placement.

## Color guidance

- Define role-based tokens: canvas, surface, elevated surface, text, muted text, border, primary action, focus, success, warning, danger, and information.
- Semantic colors should remain distinct in light and dark themes.
- Do not use color as the only indicator of state.
- Keep large background areas less saturated than interactive accents.

## Composition guidance

- Start with content hierarchy, then choose the grid.
- Use asymmetry only when it improves emphasis or rhythm.
- Avoid placing every section inside a card.
- Keep repeated components consistent; reserve visual exceptions for meaningful priority.
- Let whitespace separate concepts before adding borders and shadows.

## Common anti-patterns

- A hero section with vague copy, gradient text, and no concrete value.
- Four identical feature cards regardless of content importance.
- Excessive pills and badges that make every item look equally important.
- Rounded containers nested three levels deep.
- Low-contrast gray text used for essential information.
- Icon-only actions with ambiguous meaning.
- Mobile layouts that simply stack every desktop element in the same order.
- Decorative charts without labels, units, source, or actionable interpretation.
