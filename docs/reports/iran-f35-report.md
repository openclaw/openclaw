# Report: Analysis of the Iranian Missile Strike on Nevatim Airbase (F-35I Facilities)

## Executive Summary

On October 1, 2024, the Islamic Republic of Iran launched a massive ballistic missile barrage—codenamed "Operation True Promise II"—against the State of Israel. One of the primary strategic targets of this attack was the **Nevatim Airbase** located in the Negev desert. Nevatim is highly significant because it is the main operating base for the Israeli Air Force's (IAF) stealth fighter fleet, specifically the F-35I "Adir" squadrons.

Following the attack, Iranian state media and affiliated channels made substantial claims, asserting that the airbase was "completely destroyed" and that "up to 20 F-35 stealth fighters were destroyed" on the ground.

However, subsequent analysis utilizing commercial satellite imagery (OSINT) and statements from Israeli and independent defense analysts heavily contradicted these claims. While the airbase did sustain structural damage from multiple missile impacts, there is **zero corroborated evidence** that any F-35 fighter jets were destroyed or even damaged during the strike.

---

## The Target: Nevatim Airbase

Nevatim Airbase is one of the most critical installations for the IAF. It houses:

- **116th Squadron ("Lions of the South")**: Operating F-35I Adir.
- **140th Squadron ("Golden Eagle")**: Operating F-35I Adir.
- **117th Squadron ("First Jet")**: An F-35I training squadron.

Because the F-35I is Israel's most advanced fifth-generation fighter jet, capable of penetrating deep into heavily defended airspace (including Iran's), Nevatim is a high-priority target for Iranian ballistic missile forces.

---

## Iranian Claims vs. OSINT Reality

### The Claims

In the immediate aftermath of the October 1 barrage, Iranian military officials and state-aligned media outlets propagated claims of a devastating blow to the IAF. Reports circulated that:

- Over 80% of the missiles hit their intended targets.
- Nevatim Airbase was rendered completely inoperable.
- Up to 20 F-35 aircraft were destroyed on the tarmac or inside their hangars.

### The Verified Damage (Satellite Imagery Analysis)

In the days following the attack, independent open-source intelligence (OSINT) analysts and news organizations acquired high-resolution satellite imagery (from providers like Planet Labs and Maxar Technologies) to assess the damage.

The imagery revealed the following:

1.  **Impacts Occurred:** The base was indeed hit. Analysts counted approximately **32 missile impact craters** in and around the Nevatim facility.
2.  **Structural Damage:**
    - Missiles struck near the main runways and taxiways.
    - There was visible damage to the roof of a row of buildings near the runway, including a large hole punched through the roof of at least one aircraft hangar.
    - Debris was scattered around the affected structures.
3.  **No Aircraft Destroyed:** Crucially, **no destroyed or damaged aircraft** were visible in the satellite imagery. The hangars that were struck appeared to be empty or were housing support equipment rather than the F-35s themselves.

### IAF Counter-Measures

The survival of the F-35 fleet is attributed to standard IAF force protection protocols:

- **Airborne Dispersal:** Prior to the missile impacts (which were tracked by Israeli and allied early warning systems), the IAF scrambled its fighter fleets. The F-35s were likely airborne and safely out of the blast radius during the attack.
- **Hardened Shelters:** Any aircraft that remained on the ground would have been secured in heavily reinforced Hardened Aircraft Shelters (HAS), which are designed to withstand direct or near-direct hits from conventional warheads.

---

## Conclusion & Strategic Implications

The October 2024 Iranian missile strike succeeded in penetrating Israeli air defenses (such as the Arrow 2/3 systems) in certain areas, allowing several ballistic missiles to strike a highly defended strategic airbase. This demonstrated a capability to reach critical infrastructure.

However, the primary objective of neutralizing Israel's F-35 fleet failed completely. The claims of 20 destroyed stealth fighters were part of a psychological warfare and domestic propaganda effort by Tehran.

The continued operational status of the F-35I fleet was practically demonstrated less than a month later. On October 26, 2024, Israeli F-35s from Nevatim Airbase reportedly participated in Israel's retaliatory airstrikes against military targets deep inside Iranian territory, proving the fleet's survival and readiness.
